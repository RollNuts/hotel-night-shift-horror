# CREATIVE PILLARS

Status: active for the locked hotel night-shift horror product.

## Codex Decision Frame

- Build: fear from routine hotel work becoming uncertain.
- Do not build: lore-heavy exposition, random monster systems, broad mechanics, or disconnected scares.
- Market reason: "night shift at an old hotel" is instantly legible and easy to stream.
- Unreal reason: first-person staging, lighting, sound, monitors, door interactions, and Sequencer can make small spaces feel threatening.
- Solo-dev/low-cost reason: repeating a small set of actions in one facility creates depth without a large content catalog.
- Art/audio bar: light, sound, composition, and prop credibility must sell the game before any text explains it.
- No-small-room bar: creative approval happens in the hotel, not a blank interaction chamber.
- Steam quality bar: every pillar must strengthen screenshots, trailer beats, demo clarity, or player stories.
- Veripsa unit: creative changes must state which hotel-work action and which fear beat they improve.

## Pillar 1: Routine Is The Trap

The player must understand the job immediately. Fear comes from the same tasks changing meaning over time.

Build:

- Phone calls that start as routine requests.
- Camera checks that slowly become unreliable.
- Guest requests that can be normal, suspicious, or dangerous.
- Door decisions that feel consequential.
- Patrols that make the player learn the hotel layout.

Do not build:

- Too many unrelated item interactions.
- Abstract puzzle rules.
- Combat loops.
- Lore dumps that replace situational tension.

## Pillar 2: Every Door Is A Decision

The door is the game's most important horror object.

Build:

- Clear open/close/lock feedback.
- Reasons to hesitate.
- Sound before sight.
- Camera, phone, or report context that changes the decision.
- Consequences for opening, ignoring, or refusing.

Do not build:

- Doors as decorative props only.
- Random locked doors with no meaning.
- Cheap instant punishment without readable warning.

## Pillar 3: The Hotel Feels Real Enough To Betray You

The hotel must feel like a business hotel first, haunted/abnormal second.

Build:

- Front desk workflow.
- Back office clutter.
- Elevator and stair logic.
- Service areas.
- Repeated room doors with believable variation.
- Environmental traces that tell situation, not exposition.

Do not build:

- Oversized maze corridors.
- Unreal marketplace asset collage.
- Stylized horror symbols everywhere from the start.

## Pillar 4: Sound Leads Fear

Sound must carry much of the product value.

Build:

- Phone ring identity.
- Distant elevator and hallway cues.
- Door knocks and handle movement.
- Fluorescent hum, HVAC, rain, or street ambience.
- Different acoustic feel for lobby, corridor, stairwell, room, and back area.

Do not build:

- Silent interactions.
- Placeholder SFX in product evidence.
- Loud jumpscare mix that masks information.

## Pillar 5: UI Is Work Equipment

UI should feel like workplace tools, not a game overlay.

Build:

- Desk terminal, report log, camera monitor, phone display, clipboard, or key/lock indicators.
- Minimal prompts.
- Clear objective state.
- Diegetic or semi-diegetic information where possible.

Do not build:

- Large quest panels.
- Abstract horror meters.
- Tutorial walls.
- UI that explains what environment and sound should communicate.

## Pillar 6: Multiple Endings From Judgment, Not Content Bloat

Endings should reflect work decisions and abnormal handling.

Build:

- Endings based on who was admitted/refused, what was reported, which areas were checked, and how incidents were handled.
- Replay pressure through "I should have noticed that" moments.

Do not build:

- Long branching story trees.
- Large alternate levels.
- Collection bloat.
