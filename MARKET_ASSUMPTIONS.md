# MARKET ASSUMPTIONS

Status: genre locked to first-person, single-location, night-shift hotel horror.

## Codex Decision Frame

- Build: a small premium horror product whose store promise is "work the hotel desk, decide correctly, survive the night."
- Do not build: broad systems, action combat, open-world exploration, online play, or content volume that one developer cannot finish.
- Market reason: job-based horror is easy to explain, streamer-friendly, and can create shareable player stories from judgment mistakes.
- Unreal reason: first-person lighting, sound propagation, monitor views, Sequencer beats, and constrained spaces can sell fear without massive content.
- Solo-dev/low-cost reason: one hotel and one night keep art, QA, save design, and level scope bounded.
- Art/audio bar: the marketable quality floor is atmosphere, sound, hallway composition, readable UI, and believable hotel props.
- No-small-room bar: mechanics must be shown at the front desk, monitor station, elevator, hallway, guest door, or back-of-house route.
- Steam quality bar: a visitor must understand the work loop and horror twist from one screenshot and the first 15 seconds of video.
- Veripsa unit: track market-facing proof through screenshot candidates, trailer beats, comparable-risk notes, and review-risk mitigations.

## Commercial Hypothesis

The project can compete as a compact premium horror game if it delivers:

- A familiar job fantasy: night front desk at an old business hotel.
- A simple repeatable loop: receive request, verify, move, decide, report.
- Escalating unease: normal tasks become harder to trust.
- Strong sound: phones, fluorescent buzz, distant doors, elevator movement, hallway ambience.
- Clear player stories: "I opened the wrong door", "I ignored the camera", "I let the wrong guest in."
- Honest scope: one facility, one night, multiple endings, no promise of huge content.

## Target Steam Promise

Store visitors should immediately understand:

- You are working alone at night.
- The hotel is small enough to learn but dense enough to fear.
- The work itself creates decisions.
- The horror emerges through routine breaking, not through random feature spam.
- The game is offline and complete.

## Expected Buyer Rewards

Players should praise:

- Strong atmosphere.
- Clear job loop.
- Good sound.
- Tense door and lock decisions.
- Memorable abnormal events.
- Fair failure.
- Replayable endings.
- Clean UI.
- Stable performance.

They will likely criticize:

- Cheap-looking hotel assets.
- Weak voice/audio cues.
- Confusing objectives.
- Repetitive errands without escalation.
- Jumpscares that feel arbitrary.
- Bad checkpoints or long resets.
- Bugs that make fear funny.
- Too-short content if price is wrong.

## Market-Correct Feature Filter

Approve a feature only when it does at least one of these:

- Makes the phone, camera, patrol, door, guest, or report loop deeper.
- Creates a better screenshot inside the hotel.
- Creates a stronger 15-second trailer beat.
- Makes the player's decision clearer or more frightening.
- Creates replayable ending variation without large content bloat.
- Improves streamability or player story clarity.
- Reduces release risk.

Reject a feature when:

- It exists only because horror games sometimes have it.
- It requires a new major control scheme.
- It adds a new subsystem unrelated to hotel work.
- It needs online infrastructure.
- It makes the hotel feel like a generic maze.
- It can only be demonstrated in a test room.

## Price And Scope Assumption

Initial planning target:

- First playthrough: 2-4 hours.
- Multiple endings and replay: 5-8 hours.
- Price band: to be finalized after comparable-game review, but the product should be scoped like a polished small premium horror game rather than a long campaign.

Do not use low price to excuse unfinished atmosphere. Do not use multiple endings to hide weak moment-to-moment work.

## Comparable Research To Maintain

Keep a living comparison list before Steam page work:

- One-location horror games.
- Job-simulator horror games.
- Short premium first-person horror games.
- Steam review complaints about length, repetition, and unclear objectives.
- Trailer openings that communicate role plus fear quickly.

Use this research to tune price, demo scope, screenshot language, and store copy.
